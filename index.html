Research program

From ubiquitin mechanisms to precision degrader medicine.

We investigate how protein degradation pathways are wired in normal and malignant cells, then use that knowledge to design therapeutic strategies.

Our laboratory studies how the ubiquitin-proteasome system regulates cell proliferation, survival, and lineage-specific signaling. Cancer cells often become dependent on altered degradation programs. We define how ubiquitin ligases recognize substrates, how these pathways are rewired in disease, and how they can be exploited therapeutically.

We focus on hematologic malignancies, where dysregulated ubiquitin signaling creates selective dependencies in leukemia, lymphoma, and myeloma. By integrating biochemistry, proteomics, functional genomics, chemical biology, and in vivo disease models, we aim to convert molecular mechanisms into new therapeutic opportunities.

Research projects

Molecular research connecting mechanism, degradation, and therapy.

Our projects start from disease biology and end with experimentally testable therapeutic strategies.

The Busino Lab focuses on protein degradation pathways that create vulnerabilities in hematologic malignancies. We combine functional genetics, biochemical mechanism, molecular pharmacology, proteomics, and disease models to determine which degradation pathways are biologically important and which can be converted into druggable therapeutic concepts.

E3 ligase-substrate pairs in DLBCL and myeloma

We identify E3 ligase-substrate relationships that are relevant to diffuse large B-cell lymphoma and multiple myeloma. The goal is to understand which ubiquitin ligases control malignant cell survival, proliferation, differentiation state, immune interactions, and therapy response.

This program uses functional genetic approaches, including CRISPR-based perturbation and disease-context dependency analysis, together with biochemical and molecular assays that define substrate recognition, degron logic, ubiquitylation, protein turnover, and pathway output. We then ask whether each E3-substrate axis is mechanistically important, clinically relevant, and pharmacologically druggable.

Functional genetics · biochemistry · disease relevance

Molecular glues and PROTACs for intracellular targeted protein degradation

We discover and characterize molecular glues and PROTACs that redirect E3 ligases to eliminate intracellular disease drivers. Rather than simply inhibiting an enzymatic active site, these modalities can remove scaffold proteins, transcriptional regulators, signaling adaptors, and other proteins that are difficult to target with conventional inhibitors.

Our analysis integrates target engagement, ternary complex formation, degradation kinetics, quantitative proteomics, resistance mechanisms, and functional responses in leukemia, lymphoma, and myeloma models. The long-term goal is to define when degradation is superior to inhibition and to build rational degrader strategies for hematologic oncology.

Molecular glues · PROTACs · intracellular TPD

Lysosomal degradation to enhance cancer immunotherapy

We leverage lysosomal degradation to remove extracellular and membrane proteins that suppress anti-tumor immunity. This includes antibody-, nanobody-, and receptor-engaging degrader strategies designed to reroute surface proteins into endosomal trafficking and lysosomal destruction.

A major interest is whether durable degradation of immune checkpoints, inhibitory receptors, or disease-relevant surface proteins can improve T-cell and CAR T-cell function compared with classical blockade. We test how extracellular targeted degradation changes immune synapse biology, T-cell activation, exhaustion, tumor killing, and therapeutic selectivity in cancer models.
